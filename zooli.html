ZOOLI

Helen Li

CONTEXT

Zoos are a common destination for families enjoying a day out. With the fascinating animals, intriguing learning experiences, and even the occasional shows, zoos have become a staple of the nuclear family. However, visiting the zoo can also be a challenge for large groups. Having to cater to children of varying ages is often tiresome, thus hindering the adults’ experiences as well. Furthermore, zoo visits require extensive planning, a process that can easily be automated. In this way, Zooli steps in by providing a customized plan for any family’s zoo's visit based on varying factors like time of visit, number of children, and more.

"HOW MAY WE PLAN A ZOO VISIT THAT CATERS TO SPECIFIC FAMILIES?"

USER

RESEARCH

Acknowledging that it has been quite a while since I visited a zoo, I decided to conduct an interview to better understand the audience I would be designing for. My interviewee, John (whose name has been changed) is in his early thirties and has a wife and two children: Mary and Tyler (names changed). Mary is twelve years-old, and Tyler is seven years-old. I interviewed John about his most recent trip to the Oakland Zoo with his family. I noticed that he referenced his children’s ages a lot, saying, “At one point Mary just wanted to sit and stare at them feeding the otters, but Tyler… well Tyler’s seven, so all he wanted to do was see the biggest animals he could find.” John also mentioned his family’s route throughout the zoo, saying, “Well the zoo, it has a main area and then another place you go to on a gondola. We didn’t know what Mary and Tyler would want to see or eat, so we just ended up riding the gondola like four times back and forth.” John’s interview enlightened me to the various logistical problems involved in planning a zoo trip for multiple people.

PRODUCT

LANDSCAPE

I next conducted a product landscape on mobile applications for local zoos. The two applications I focused on were the San Francisco Zoo mobile application and the Oakland Zoo mobile application because these were the only two zoos I had personally visited in the last five years. For either application, I constructed a “plusses and deltas” list to pinpoint how they were relevant to the concerns raised in John’s interview.

SAN FRANCISCO ZOO MOBILE APPLICATION

PLUSSES

A map with a suggested path that took me to all the exhibits

Buttons that instantly toggled me to certain exhibits as well as bathrooms and gift shops

A scavenger hunt that asked me trivia questions about the exhibits

DELTAS

The scavenger hunt did not match the path suggested on the map

The map did not include relevant information about special events (I had to toggle back and forth between two screens)

The suggested route itself was based more on efficiency than interest or relevancy

OAKLAND ZOO MOBILE APPLICATION

PLUSSES

Inclusion of important amenities like water fountains and stroller parking

Pictures of the animals, categorized by exhibit, with interesting facts

A calendar of events with the option to “favorite” them

DELTAS

No interactive elements on the map (ie. no suggested route, buttons, etc.)

No options on the map to view day-to-day events, like feedings and animal shows

No information about gift shops on the map

IDEATION

I took the data gathered from my interview with John and my product landscape to generate various insights that I then organized into two main categories: “path-planning and “catering to many.” By organizing these insights, I eventually decided on a mobile application for a (hypothetical) zoo that could generate a custom route for the visiting family. I knew this application would be heavily dependent on personal customization, so I also used my gathered insights to decide which attributes would be used to generate the different paths through the zoo.

SKETCHING

After consolidating my data and generating insights, I moved on to sketching some initial ideas for my mobile application. The following wireframes are just some of the results.

To provide a seamless experience, I chose to take advantage of some of iOS’s design patterns. Here, I mimic the iPhone’s dropdown picker when asking the user how many children he or she will have.

I wanted to provide the user at least some power in case the family had at least one exhibit that was necessary to visit. To be more informational, these photographs of the exhibits, when clicked, reveal further information about the animal.

Rather than providing just one customized path for the user, I believed that giving several would give the user more power. Each path is provided in list format and can be clicked on for more information. An arrow button at the bottom right will take the user to a map of the zoo with the path outlined on it.

MarvelApp Prototype

I opted to utilize standard iOS designs in my mobile application, making Zooli more intuitive for the user. Furthermore, I noticed that while I was designing the simpler questions for my user to answer, the interfaces looked very similar to one another and could be confusing. Thus, I opted to highlight each topical word in the question in blue.

In the checklists for exhibits and events to choose, the user indicates which items to prioritize with a checkbox. When implementing the checklists, I knew that the user might not be fully fluent with what each item entailed. Therefore, when clicked, each option drops down a small square of information that simply pushes down the items below it by one box.

At the end of the survey, Zooli asks for any special accomodations. This was done in an attempt to cater to as many needs as possible. This includes items ranging from wheelchair access to lunch and dinner areas. In this way, Zooli becomes a more generalized mobile application that can apply to multiple zoos while also catering to different kinds of families.

The results page displays all available paths that match the preferences of the user. Each path has a descriptive title as well as a short description. When clicked, the item toggles down to provide more information as well as exactly how closely the path matches the user's preferences. There is also an arrow that brings the user to a map of the zoo with the path overlaid.

Finally, after choosing a path, Zooli reveals a map of the zoo with the path overlaid on top. There is a small indicator on the map that tracks where the user is, and based on location, interesting facts pop up at the bottom of the screen that match the theme of the path as well as the location.

NEXT UP

If this were a a full design sprint, I would have invested more time into user research by finding more interviewees. This could help me form some personas and storyboards to better understand my audience. I would also perform some usability tests to gather more insights that could help with future iterations. Finally, I would try prototyping it with an actual existing zoo rather than just a hypothetical one.
